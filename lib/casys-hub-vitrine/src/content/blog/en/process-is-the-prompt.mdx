# What If AI Just Needed a Process?

> I reinvented process management — without realizing it. And it works.

---

**I tried OpenClaw last week.** The open-source bridge that connects Claude to WhatsApp, Telegram and Signal — the one we discussed in [the last post](/blog/ai-moves-to-messaging). 135,000 GitHub stars in three months. Time to test it.

Three hours later, I was no longer in OpenClaw. I was in [Windmill](https://www.windmill.dev/) — a workflow orchestrator — structuring pipelines for my agents.

That's when I had a moment of déjà vu.

What I was doing, I'd seen before. Not in a GitHub repo. In an engineering school class, ten years ago.

I was giving my agents **documentation**. I was assigning them **tools**. I was building specialized **teams**. I was measuring their **performance**.

Without thinking about it, without planning it, I was reproducing what I'd been taught in quality management.

---

## The Turtle Diagram

There's a tool that's been used in industry since the 1980s to describe any process. It's called the **Turtle Diagram** — probably because it vaguely looks like a turtle seen from above, with its four legs. It's a pillar of quality management, popularized by Philip Crosby in his "zero defects" approach.

The idea is simple. For any process, you ask four questions:

- **With what?** — The tools and equipment needed
- **With whom?** — The skills and roles
- **How?** — The procedures, the documentation
- **How much?** — The performance indicators

In the center: the process itself. On the left: inputs. On the right: outputs.

It's a thing from white-collar workers with binders. Factories. Production lines. Nothing to do with AI.

And yet.

---

## The Same Shape, Everywhere

Here's what stopped me. When you put AI agents in a workflow, you find exactly the same boxes.

| Turtle Diagram | Agent Orchestration |
| --- | --- |
| **With what** — machines, software | MCP tools, APIs, function calling |
| **With whom** — trained staff | Specialized agents, assigned by task |
| **How** — procedures, documentation | System prompts, context files, CLAUDE.md |
| **How much** — conformity rate | Tokens consumed, latency, hallucination rate |
| **Inputs** — raw materials | User intent, conversational context |
| **Outputs** — conforming product | Structured result, executed action |

The correspondence is striking. Not because it's forced — because it's *natural*. Nobody sat down and said "let's apply quality management to LLMs." It happened on its own. Everyone who's seriously tried to get agents working in a workflow has converged on this pattern — without knowing it.

The same shape emerges regardless of context. Because it's the minimal structure for work to be done correctly by an entity that follows instructions — whether it's made of flesh or silicon.

---

## The Economy of Speech

An agent without a process is an LLM with its entire latent space as playground. It can go anywhere. And that's exactly what it does. It compensates for the lack of structure with verbosity — it "covers its bases" by generating more, like an intern without a job description who does a bit of everything to look useful.

An agent in a process is an LLM whose space is constrained. It knows what to do, with what, and how the result is measured. It produces fewer tokens. More relevant ones.

David Byrne sang in 1977: *"You're talking a lot, but you're not saying anything."* He wasn't talking about LLMs. But the line captures the phenomenon exactly. A verbose agent isn't a stupid agent. It's an agent **without process constraints**.

Wasted tokens. Hallucinations that need checking. Rework iterations when the first output was off-topic. These aren't intelligence problems. They're process problems.

And process is free. The cost of setting it up is always less than the cost of its absence.

## Orchestrators Won't Say Their Name

When I stepped back and looked at what Windmill was making me do, I realized that all these tools — Windmill, n8n, Make, the rest — are actually **process management tools** disguised as automation tools. What they do is BPMN — Business Process Model and Notation — the standard for modeling business processes. The same boxes, the same arrows, the same gateways. Except instead of drawing swimlanes for humans, you're drawing swimlanes for agents.

Look at what they ask you to do when you integrate an agent:

1. **Document** the context and instructions
2. **List** the available tools and permissions
3. **Assign** an agent to each step
4. **Define** success and failure criteria
5. **Iterate** when the output isn't right

That's quality management. Dressed in YAML.

And specialization works here like everywhere else: a pipeline of five specialized agents consistently outperforms a generalist agent trying to do everything in a single prompt.

## Crosby's Five Levels

Crosby formalized a five-stage maturity grid to describe how an organization learns to master quality — from total uncertainty to reflex. When I map the teams I know and their relationship with agents, I find exactly the same levels.

| Maturity | Human Process (Crosby) | Agent Orchestration |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **1 — Uncertainty** | "We have no process" | "We copy-paste prompts into ChatGPT" |
| **2 — Awakening** | "We're starting to document" | "We have a system prompt and a few tools" |
| **3 — Enlightenment** | "We measure and improve" | "Structured workflows with monitoring" |
| **4 — Wisdom** | "Process is part of the culture" | "A CI/CD pipeline for orchestration" |
| **5 — Certainty** | "Quality is a reflex" | "Self-evaluating agents, adaptive workflows" |

<ExcalidrawDiagram src="process-maturity-agents" alt="Crosby's Quality Maturity Grid → Agent Orchestration Maturity" />

Most people I know are between level 1 and level 2. Those building serious products with agents are at level 3. Level 5, I haven't seen anywhere yet.

A few observations from experience:

**Too much process kills the process.** Too much context saturates the context window. Performance degrades. The optimal process isn't the maximal process — it's the *sufficient* process. A "good enough" that applies not to [personality](/blog/personality-is-just-friction), but to structure.

**Too much specialization creates fragility.** A workflow of hyper-specialized agents breaks the moment one step fails. Resilience comes from the balance between specialization and adaptability.

**None of this is new.** It's been documented for decades. We don't need to invent new theories to run agents. We need to reread what we already knew.

---

## The Question Nobody's Asking

Why is nobody talking about this?

Maybe because "agents work better when you give them a process" is a profoundly boring conclusion. It's not a paper that makes the front page of Hacker News. It's not a spectacular benchmark. It's not a new model with a five-letter acronym.

**Structure beats talent.** An average model in a good process outperforms an excellent model without one. It's true for humans. It's even more true for agents — because agents *are* processes. They execute instructions. They consume inputs. They produce outputs. The Turtle Diagram isn't a metaphor for them. It's a **specification**.

---

## Quality Is Free. Tokens Are Not.

What struck me coming back from my Windmill detour wasn't that agents "need management." It's that **the structure of well-done work doesn't depend on who does it** — human, robot, AI agent. It depends on the nature of the work itself.

In 2026, the translation is clear:

- The cost of a well-structured workflow: a few hours of design
- The cost of an agent without a process: thousands of wasted tokens, hallucinations to verify, human rework to fix the output

Models change. Frameworks change. Tools change. But the structure of well-done work doesn't.

Quality engineers knew this in the 1980s. We just rediscovered it with GPUs.
